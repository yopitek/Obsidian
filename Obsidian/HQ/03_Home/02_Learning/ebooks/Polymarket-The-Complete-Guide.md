§01  Prediction Markets: Truth Machines

Powered by Money

Prediction Markets: Truth Machines Powered by Money

Polls  say  one  thing,  experts  say  another,  and  social  media  says  something  else  entirely.  The  more

information we have, the harder it becomes to tell what's actually true. Is there a way to aggregate

the scattered fragments of knowledge inside millions of people's heads into a single number that's

closest to the truth?

How Much Does a Bull Weigh?

In 1907, at a county fair in Plymouth, England, the statistician Francis Galton conducted an experiment that

has been cited countless times since.

A bull was led to the center of the fairground, and 787 people — butchers, farmers, and passersby with zero

bovine  expertise  —  each  paid  sixpence  to  guess  the  animal's  dressed  weight  after  slaughter.  Galton,  an

advocate of eugenics who didn't think much of ordinary people's intelligence, fully expected the crowd to get

it wrong.

The result stunned even him: the median of all 787 guesses was 1,207 pounds. The bull's actual weight was

1,198 pounds. The error was less than 1%.

No  single  individual's  guess  beat  the  crowd's  median.  Not  even  the  butchers  —  supposedly  the  experts  —

came closer.

This is the classic case of the "wisdom of crowds": when enough independent judgments are pooled

together, random errors cancel each other out and converge on a number astonishingly close to the truth.

A century later, journalist James Surowiecki turned this principle into the bestseller The Wisdom of Crowds,

identifying four conditions for collective intelligence to work: diversity, independence, decentralization, and

an effective aggregation mechanism.

The question is: what's the best "aggregation mechanism"?

Price as an Information Compression Engine

In  1945,  Austrian  economist  Friedrich  Hayek  published  a  paper  barely  a  dozen  pages  long:  "The  Use  of

Knowledge in Society." It would come to be regarded as one of the most important economics papers of the

20th century and a cornerstone of Hayek's Nobel Prize.

Hayek's  core  insight  was  simple:  no  single  person  —  however  brilliant  —  can  possess  all  the  dispersed

knowledge  in  society.  A  tin  miner  knows  the  state  of  the  ore  veins.  A  shipping  company  owner  knows  the

logistics  bottlenecks.  A  factory  manager  knows  the  capacity  limits.  These  fragments  of  knowledge  are

scattered across millions of minds, and no central planning committee can ever collect them all.

But market prices can.

When  tin  output  falls,  the  price  of  tin  rises.  That  price  signal  propagates  along  the  entire  supply  chain:

factories  cut  their  tin  purchases,  their  customers  seek  substitutes,  consumers  shift  to  tin-free  products.

Millions of people, without knowing why tin got expensive, make the right adjustments.

Key Insight

Hayek's insight can be boiled down to one sentence: Price is the most efficient information aggregator

ever invented by humanity. It compresses the dispersed knowledge of countless people into a single

number that anyone can read and act on.

Prediction  markets  apply  this  same  principle  to  the  probabilities  of  future  events.  When  you  buy  a  "Trump

wins  the  2024  election"  contract  on  a  prediction  market,  you're  not  merely  expressing  an  opinion  —  you're

backing your judgment with real money. If you have insider information, a better model, or just an intuitive

read on a particular swing district, that information flows into the market price through your trade.

Aggregate  thousands  of  such  trades,  and  the  market  price  becomes  a  real-time,  continuously  updated

probability estimate.

From Papal Bets to Presidential Elections: A Brief History

Prediction markets have a much longer history than most people realize.

In  16th-century  Italy  and  Ireland,  betting  pools  emerged  around  papal  elections.  People  wagered  on  who

would  become  the  next  pope,  with  odds  reflecting  each  cardinal's  chances.  Given  the  Catholic  Church's

stance on gambling, the irony is hard to miss.

By the 19th and early 20th centuries in America, Wall Street "election pools" were practically a fixture of every

presidential race. New York traders bet real money on election outcomes outside the formal exchanges. From

1884 to 1940, these betting pools correctly predicted the winner in 11 out of 15 presidential elections.

The modern prediction market truly began in 1988. A group of professors at the University of Iowa's business

school created the Iowa Electronic Markets (IEM), the first academic prediction market platform. IEM received

a special no-action letter from the CFTC allowing small-scale real-money trading (capped at $500 per person),

specifically for election prediction research.

IEM's  track  record  was  impressive:  over  many  years,  its  election  predictions  consistently  outperformed

contemporaneous  polling  data.  This  finding  attracted  significant  academic  attention  and  spawned  more

prediction market platforms.

In 2001, economist Robin Hanson proposed an even more radical idea: why not use prediction markets for all

major  decisions?  He  coined  the  term  "futarchy"  —  governance  by  prediction  market.  In  a  futarchy,  citizens

vote on goals (e.g., maximize GDP growth), and prediction markets determine methods (which policy is most

likely to achieve the goal).

No  country  has  adopted  this  idea,  but  it  profoundly  influenced  later  prediction  market  entrepreneurs,

including Polymarket founder Shayne Coplan.

The  2024  U.S.  presidential  election  was  the  ultimate  real-world  test.  Before  the  election,  virtually  all  major

Why "Voting with Money" Beats "Voting with Words"

It  all  comes  down  to  one  thing:  when  you  have  to  put  your  own  money  on  the  line,  you  take  it  a  lot  more

seriously than answering a survey question.

The problem with polls is that respondents have zero incentive to tell the truth. You might give an inaccurate

answer because of embarrassment, social pressure, or just for fun. This is the so-called "shy voter" effect —

many Trump supporters in both 2016 and 2024 concealed their true preferences from pollsters.

Experts  have  a  different  problem.  They  face  reputational  pressure,  but  that  pressure  actually  encourages

conformity:  if  all  your  peers  say  A  will  win  and  you  alone  predict  B,  you  get  your  reputation  destroyed  if

wrong,  and  if  right,  people  just  say  you  "got  lucky."  So  expert  predictions  tend  to  converge  on  the  safe

consensus.

Prediction  markets  flip  this  dynamic.  Think  the  market  severely  underestimates  a  certain  outcome?  Buy

cheap contracts in bulk — if you're right, the profit is substantial. This mechanism naturally attracts people

with  exclusive  information  or  superior  analytical  ability  —  the  "smart  money"  —  and  their  trading

continuously corrects the market price, pushing it closer to the true probability.

Economists call this the "marginal trader hypothesis": it's not the average participant that determines

the market price, but the most informed, most active marginal traders. Most people may know nothing

about an event, but as long as a few informed individuals are willing to bet, the price gets pushed in the right

direction.

Of  course,  prediction  markets  aren't  infallible.  They  perform  well  under  certain  conditions:  enough

participants,  sufficient  liquidity,  and  information  that  can  be  gathered  in  a  decentralized  fashion.  When

participation is too thin (say, on an obscure topic), or information is highly concentrated in a few hands (like

cryptocurrency insider knowledge), accuracy drops off a cliff.

The May 2025 papal election was a textbook counter-example. Polymarket gave the eventual winner, Robert

Prevost,  only  a  0.3%  probability,  while  the  frontrunner  Pietro  Parolin  stood  at  just  28%.  The  reason  was

simple:  information  from  the  conclave  was  hermetically  sealed,  rendering  the  market's  information

aggregation mechanism useless.

Warning

Prediction markets are not crystal balls. They are information aggregators, and the quality of their output

depends on the quality and quantity of the information fed into them. In high-attention, high-liquidity

markets (like the U.S. presidential election), they are among the most reliable forecasting tools available. In

markets with closed information and sparse participation, they may be barely better than a coin flip.

Now that we understand the principles and limitations of prediction markets, let's look at how a 22-year-old

college dropout turned this academic concept into a commercial empire valued at nearly ten billion dollars.

Polymarket: A Dropout's Twenty-Billion-Dollar Prediction Empire

A  teenager  from  Manhattan's  Upper  West  Side  bought  Ethereum  at  16,  wrote  a  letter  to  an

Starting with $0.30 Ethereum

Shayne Coplan was born in 1998 and grew up on Manhattan's Upper West Side. The entrepreneurial instinct

showed  early:  in  2014,  at  16,  Coplan  participated  in  Ethereum's  ICO  (Initial  Coin  Offering),  buying  ETH  at

roughly $0.30 a token. At today's prices, that investment's return exceeds 10,000x.

While still in high school, Coplan attempted to build an ECN (Electronic Communication Network) exchange

and even proactively contacted the SEC to ask about regulatory requirements. A high schooler reaching out

to  the  U.S.  Securities  and  Exchange  Commission  to  discuss  compliance  reveals  a  rather  unusual  character

trait: his interest in financial markets and regulation wasn't rebellious — it was earnest and constructive.

After  enrolling  at  New  York  University  (NYU)  to  study  computer  science,  Coplan  encountered  two  thinkers

who would profoundly shape his future venture: Friedrich Hayek and Robin Hanson.

Hayek's  information  theory  (prices  as  aggregators  of  dispersed  knowledge)  was  covered  in  the  previous

chapter. George Mason University professor Robin Hanson went further: he proposed "futarchy," the idea of

using prediction markets to supplement or even replace traditional democratic voting for major decisions.

In 2019, 21-year-old Coplan wrote a letter to Robin Hanson saying he wanted to bring prediction markets to

the  mainstream.  Hanson's  reply  was  lukewarm  —  he'd  seen  too  many  young  people  claim  they'd  make

prediction markets mainstream, and none had succeeded. But Coplan wasn't deterred.

In June 2020, with COVID raging worldwide, Coplan dropped out of NYU and began coding alone in his Lower

East Side apartment. The initial platform was called Union Market, but it quickly pivoted toward prediction

markets. The pandemic had created so much uncertainty that people desperately needed a more reliable way

to understand what was happening than news headlines.

The platform was renamed Polymarket. Coplan was 22.

Product Mechanics: Turning Probabilities into Tradeable Assets

Polymarket's product logic is straightforward — it can be explained in one sentence: you pay money to buy

"Yes" or "No," and if you're right, you get $1 back; if you're wrong, you get zero.

Let's break it down with a concrete example.

Example: "Will Bitcoin break $100,000 by end of March 2026?"

This market has two tradeable contracts: Yes shares and No shares. If you think Bitcoin will break $100K,

you buy Yes shares; if you think it won't, you buy No shares.

Suppose the current price of a Yes share is $0.65 — meaning the market consensus puts the probability at

65%.

If Bitcoin does break $100K, your Yes share is worth $1.00 — you've earned $0.35 (54% return). If it doesn't,

your Yes share goes to zero — you lose $0.65.

The  key  design  here  is:  price  equals  probability.  A  $0.65  Yes  share  =  a  65%  probability  estimate.  Nobody

decrees  this  —  it  emerges  naturally  from  supply  and  demand.  For  instance,  if  bullish  news  breaks

(institutional buying spree), more people rush to buy Yes shares, the price rises to $0.80, and the probability

estimate updates to 80%.

Every price movement is an information update.

You don't have to wait until the event resolves to exit. If you bought Yes shares at $0.40 and the price rises to

$0.75,  you  can  simply  sell  and  take  profits  —  not  unlike  trading  stocks.  This  flexibility  makes  prediction

markets not just a casino, but something closer to an information exchange.

Settlement: Who Determines the "Truth"?

The most critical trust issue for any prediction market platform is: who decides the outcome when the event

concludes?

Polymarket  uses  UMA  Protocol's  decentralized  oracle.  When  a  market  expires,  UMA's  oracle  determines  the

result based on predefined settlement criteria (e.g., "BTC/USD price on CoinGecko"). If there's a dispute, UMA

token holders can vote to adjudicate.

This design avoids the centralization risk of "the platform decides." In theory, no single entity can manipulate

settlement outcomes.

Let's unpack each layer.

Blockchain layer: Polygon network. Polymarket runs on Polygon (an Ethereum Layer 2 scaling network) for

practical  reasons:  low  gas  fees,  fast  transactions,  and  full  compatibility  with  the  Ethereum  ecosystem.  For

users, transaction costs are negligible.

Settlement  token:  USDC.  All  trades  are  denominated  and  settled  in  USDC  (a  USD  stablecoin).  This  is  a

deliberate choice — by not using a native platform token, it lowers the learning curve for new users. Think of

USDC as "dollars on-chain."

Order  book:  Hybrid  Central  Limit  Order  Book  (CLOB).  This  is  the  most  elegant  part  of  the  architecture.

Unlike most DeFi projects that use AMMs (Automated Market Makers), Polymarket uses the traditional order

book model: buyers and sellers post orders and match directly, rather than trading against a liquidity pool.

Key Insight

Why not AMM? Because prediction market contracts can only resolve to 0 or 1, AMMs are extremely

inefficient for this binary structure. An order book provides tighter bid-ask spreads, making trades cheaper

and liquidity deeper.

"Hybrid"  means:  order  matching  happens  off-chain  (on  backend  servers)  for  speed  and  user  experience,

while actual fund settlement and token delivery happen on-chain (via smart contracts) for transparency and

verifiability. It's a pragmatic compromise. A purely on-chain order book would be too slow; purely off-chain

would sacrifice blockchain's trust advantages.

Smart contract layer: Gnosis Conditional Tokens Framework. The Yes/No shares users buy are technically

ERC-1155 tokens minted via Gnosis's conditional token framework. Deposit 1 USDC, and the smart contract

mints 1 Yes token and 1 No token, whose combined value always equals 1 USDC.

4.5 Trading: Price Equals Probability

Understanding Polymarket comes down to one sentence: price equals probability.

Example: A price of $0.65 = the market believes there's a 65% chance the event occurs. If you buy YES at

$0.65 and the event happens, you receive $1.00 (net gain $0.35). If it doesn't happen, you lose $0.65. Buying

NO at $0.35 works symmetrically.

Every market has "Yes" and "No" sides, with prices ranging from $0 to $1. You can sell your position at any

time before settlement.

Market Order

Executes  immediately  at  the  current  best  price.  Guarantees  execution  but  may  involve  slippage.  Simple  to

use:  select  market  ->  choose  Yes/No  ->  enter  amount  ->  confirm.  Best  when  you  want  to  enter  a  position

quickly and don't mind a few cents of price difference.

Limit Order

Set a desired price and wait for the market to reach it. No guarantee of execution, but you get a better price.

Supports partial fills and expiration times. Process: select market -> choose Yes/No -> switch to Limit -> enter

price and quantity -> place order.

Selecting Wallets by Total Profit Alone

A  wallet  showing  $400K  in  total  profit  might  owe  it  all  to  a  single  "chosen  one"  bet  during  the  2024

The Basket Method: Best Copy-Trading Practice

After all those pitfalls, can copy-trading actually work? Yes, but you need the right approach.

Basket Method Workflow

Step 1: Select 5-10 high-profit wallets that are active in your domain of interest and have recently stable

Step 2: Monitor them using PolyTrack or Polywhaler

Step 3: Only consider entering when 80% or more (e.g., 4 out of 5) independently enter the same market

Step 4: Limit each trade to 5-10% of total capital, while maintaining 20-40% cash reserves

Step 5: Periodically (monthly) review each wallet in your basket, drop underperformers, and add new ones

Why  is  the  basket  method  better  than  copying  individuals?  Because  it  leverages  "collective  wisdom"  rather

but the probability of five independent judges all making the same mistake is much lower.

One  more  important  distinction:  prioritize  "profit  leaders"  over  "volume  leaders."  High  volume  only

means frequent trading or large capital — it doesn't imply genuine directional edge. Consistent profit growth

is the real evidence of judgment.

Strategy 2: Cross-Platform Arbitrage

The lowest barrier and simplest logic of all six strategies.

Principle: the same event may be priced differently across prediction market platforms. If the prices on two

platforms sum to less than $1, you profit from the spread regardless of the outcome.

Example: BTC Price Prediction

Polymarket "BTC breaks $100K by year-end" Yes share = $0.45

Kalshi same event No share = $0.48

Buy both simultaneously, cost = $0.93

Regardless of whether BTC breaks $100K, one side always settles at $1.00.

Locked-in profit = $1.00 - $0.93 = $0.07 (7.5% risk-free return)

From April 2024 to April 2025, cross-platform arbitrage traders earned over $40 million in total, with the top

three wallets earning $4.2 million combined.

Risk:  Appears  "risk-free,"  but  there's  one  critical  trap:  different  platforms  may  use  different  settlement

definitions.  For  example,  Polymarket  uses  CoinGecko  prices  while  Kalshi  uses  CME  futures  prices  —  during

extreme volatility, these can yield different settlement results, breaking the hedge protection.

Barrier  to  entry:  Lowest.  Requires  accounts  on  multiple  platforms  (at  least  Polymarket  +  Kalshi),  basic

capital, and enough patience to monitor price discrepancies.

Principle: buy "near-certain" event shares priced above $0.95 and hold them like short-term bonds. When the

just a few days, the annualized return is extraordinary.

Example: Federal Reserve Rate Decision

Four-Layer Architecture: How Bots Make Decisions

Regardless of which bot, the underlying architecture is broadly similar and can be understood in four layers.

This is the core technical content of AI trading bots.

1

4

Data Layer: Eyes and Ears

Bots  must  simultaneously  monitor  multiple  data  sources:  Polymarket's  Gamma  API  (market  listings

and  live  odds),  CLOB  WebSocket  (real-time  order  flow),  Polygon  on-chain  data  (wallet  balances  and

better the bot, the more data sources it taps and the faster it reacts.

Decision Layer: The Brain

The mainstream approach is multi-model ensemble voting. Multiple LLMs independently predict event

probabilities,  and  results  are  aggregated  via  trimmed  mean  or  weighted  average,  with  weights

dynamically adjusted by historical Brier score — whichever model predicts more accurately for a given

category gets more say.

There  are  four  decision  types:  Probability  deviation  trading  (market  prices  at  40%  but  model  says

(real-time news -> AI impact analysis -> trade before repricing); Hedge discovery (LLM analyzes logical

relationships between markets, seeking inverse position combinations).

Execution Layer: Hands and Feet

Signed orders are submitted via the CLOB (note: the feeRateBps field must be included, or the order is

rejected  outright).  Mature  bots  select  execution  strategies  based  on  order  size:  Simple  (single  limit

order)  for  small  orders,  TWAP  (time-weighted  slicing  to  avoid  market  impact)  for  large  orders,  and

Iceberg (showing only 20% of true order volume) for very large orders.

Risk Control Layer: The Brakes

This  layer  determines  whether  a  bot  is  a  money-making  tool  or  a  money-losing  machine.  Standard

features  of  mature  bots  include:  kill  switch  (one-click  halt  all  trading),  auto  stop-loss  at  20%  max

drawdown, four-tier intensity system (Normal -> Warning -> Critical -> Max), per-market position limits,

daily  loss  caps,  and  a  minimum  4%  net  margin  requirement  (don't  trade  if  post-fee  return  is  below

Introduction  of  Dynamic  Taker  Fees.  15-minute  and  5-minute  crypto  markets  now  charge  taker  fees,

the  profit  margin  of  high-frequency  arbitrage  —  if  your  strategy's  edge  is  only  1%,  subtracting  a  1.56%  fee

means you're losing money.

200ms Threshold. If a market maker's cancel-and-repost interval exceeds 200 milliseconds, others can fill at

the stale price within that window, creating adverse selection risk. This forces market-making bots to update

prices within 200ms.

Nobody can prove what the account holder knew in advance. But the combination of "new account + precise

timing + massive one-directional bet" is widely considered to exhibit the hallmarks of insider trading. If the

same thing had happened on the New York Stock Exchange, the SEC would have opened an investigation that

same day.

When the "Truth Machine" Gets Gamed: The UMA Oracle Incident

In March 2025, Polymarket experienced its most serious trust crisis since inception.

It happened on a seemingly ordinary market: "Will Ukraine agree to Trump's mineral deal before April?" Total

volume:  $7  million  —  not  especially  large.  But  the  problem  it  exposed  was  enough  to  shake  the  platform's

very foundations.

At  the  settlement  deadline,  no  agreement  had  been  formally  signed.  By  all  logic,  this  market  should  have

settled as "No." But what happened next took everyone by surprise.

Polymarket  uses  the  UMA  protocol  as  its  oracle  —  essentially,  decentralized  voting  to  determine  "whether

something  happened  or  not."  The  problem:  whoever  holds  UMA  tokens  has  voting  power.  And  the

distribution of UMA tokens is extremely concentrated.

This  incident  exposed  a  fatal  structural  flaw:  manipulating  a  UMA  vote  requires  holding  a  large  amount  of

Spending $20 million to lever $1.4 billion creates a severely asymmetric cost-benefit equation.

the voting threshold to 65% and restricted settlement proposals to whitelisted addresses. But the structural

problem remains unresolved.

How $2 Million Was Lost

Here's  a  real  case  study.  This  trader  made  53  trades  with  a  51%  win  rate  —  winning  27,  losing  26.  Sounds

roughly break-even? But they lost over $2 million.

Three fatal mistakes.

First, oversized positions. Allocating a large share of capital to single events. Wins earned a little; losses cost a

fortune.

Second, poor entry prices. The vast majority of trades were buying Yes shares in the $0.51-$0.67 range. Buying

Yes at $0.60: win earns 67%, loss costs 100%. The odds are inherently stacked against you. This is actually the

most  treacherous  entry  zone  on  Polymarket:  it  feels  like  "probably  going  to  win,"  but  the  payoff  math  is

terrible.

Third, zero stop-losses. Every position was held to settlement, even as probabilities deteriorated. Polymarket

shares can be sold before settlement, but this trader chose to hold through every loss.

The root cause was a cognitive error: interpreting a $0.66 Yes price as "66% chance of winning, probably fine,"

instead of "Is the true probability higher than 66%? Only if it is, does buying at this price make mathematical

sense."

Risk Management: Surviving Is the Prerequisite for Earning

Key Insight

Position Iron Rule: No single event should exceed 3-5% of total capital. Had the $2M loss case above

followed a 5% cap, the maximum loss would have been $100K. This rule sounds conservative, but it's the

single biggest differentiator between professional traders and amateurs.

Key Insight

Entry Discipline: Don't casually enter at Yes prices above $0.55, unless you truly have an information edge

on the event. Above $0.65 requires extremely strong conviction. Buying Yes at $0.65 — win earns 54%, loss

costs 100% — you need more than 65% confidence for this trade to have positive expected value.

Key Insight

Dynamic Stop-Loss and Take-Profit: Don't hold to settlement no matter what. When new information

causes a significant probability shift, adjust your position decisively. Take profits in tranches on winners; cut

losers early. Polymarket shares can be bought and sold at any time — not leveraging this flexibility is leaving

money on the table.

Key Insight

Stay Within Your Competence: Only trade in domains you understand. If you know AI, focus on AI markets.

If you follow American politics, do political markets. It's better to skip domains you don't understand than to

chase hot topics.

Polymarket's Response: Insider Trading Rule Update

Facing  a  string  of  scandals,  Polymarket  updated  its  insider  trading  rules  on  March  23,  2026.  The  new  rules

established three red lines:

Trading  on  stolen  classified  information  is  prohibited.  Cases  like  the  Israeli  military  leak  now  have  an

explicit rule basis.

Trading on illegally obtained information is prohibited. Broader in scope — not just "stolen" information,

but any information obtained through illegal channels.

People  who  can  influence  outcomes  are  prohibited  from  trading.  If  you  are  a  decision-maker  (e.g.,  a

government  official),  you  cannot  bet  on  related  markets.  Congressman  Seth  Moulton  has  already  taken  the

lead, prohibiting his staff from using prediction markets.

Rules  are  in  place,  but  enforcement  is  another  matter.  On  a  decentralized  on-chain  platform,  how  do  you

identify "insider information"? How do you trace the real person behind an anonymous wallet? Polymarket

has  deployed  Palantir's  AI  monitoring  system  (discussed  in  detail  in  §09),  but  whether  technical  tools  can

keep pace with arbitrageurs' creativity remains an open question.

The Core Tension: Decentralization vs. Credibility

Stringing these incidents together, Polymarket faces a fundamental identity crisis.

It  wants  to  be  two  things  simultaneously:  a  "decentralized  truth-discovery  mechanism"  that  uses  market

forces and on-chain governance to determine facts; and a "regulated financial product" that operates legally

in the U.S. under CFTC oversight, attracting mainstream users and institutional capital.

But these two goals are inherently contradictory.

The UMA incident proved how fragile decentralized governance can be. The Israeli and Maduro cases proved

how  anonymity  can  be  exploited  by  intelligence  operatives.  Columbia's  research  showed  how  inflated  the

volume data is. The 92.4% losing wallet rate reveals the true situation for retail participants.

Every  single  problem  pushes  Polymarket  toward  more  centralized  control.  More  KYC,  stricter  monitoring,

more  concentrated  settlement  authority.  These  measures  may  indeed  reduce  manipulation  and  insider

trading, but they also incrementally erode "decentralization" — the original value proposition.

A final word: Discussing these dark sides isn't to discourage you from participating. It's to ensure you know

the complete rules of the game before you enter. Oracles can be manipulated, military secrets can become

betting chips, a quarter of the volume may be fake, 92% of participants are destined to lose, and regulations

could shift at any moment. Knowing all this and still choosing to participate — at least you're doing so with

clear eyes.

Regulatory Landscape: A Three-Way Game

The  most  critical  challenge  facing  prediction  markets  isn't  technology  or  user  growth  —  it's  an  existential

classification battle: what exactly is it?

Regulatory  changes  in  2025-2026  can  be  summed  up  in  one  sentence:  the  federal  level  is  opening  the

floodgates, while the state level and Congress are tightening the screws.

Federal: Opening the Gates

In November 2025, the CFTC formally approved Polymarket's return to the U.S. market, issuing an Amended

Order  of  Designation.  Requirements  include  enhanced  monitoring,  clearing  procedures,  and  full  Part  16

reporting — but the door is open. The CFTC had previously classified prediction markets as "swaps," placing

them under exclusive federal jurisdiction. This theoretically shields prediction markets from many state-level

gambling bans.

Nevada: The First Shot

The Nevada Gaming Control Board (NGCB) became the first state to sue Polymarket. In February 2026, a judge

issued  a  temporary  restraining  order  (TRO)  outright  prohibiting  Polymarket  from  operating  in  Nevada.  The

rationale:  Polymarket's  event  contracts  constitute  "unlicensed  gaming."  Kalshi  and  Polymarket  filed  a  joint

appeal,  and  the  Trump  administration  voiced  support  for  both  platforms.  But  the  TRO's  very  existence  is  a

signal: states aren't giving up jurisdiction without a fight.

Congress: The Biggest Threat

In March 2026, Senators Adam Schiff (D-CA) and John Curtis (R-UT) jointly introduced the "Prediction Markets

are  Gambling  Act."  The  bill's  content  is  blunt:  ban  prediction  market  contracts  involving  elections,

government actions, war, and sports.

These four categories combined basically cover the entire core business of Polymarket and Kalshi.

Bipartisan sponsorship means this isn't political posturing from one side — there's genuine legislative intent.

If passed, the entire industry's business model would need to be rebuilt from scratch. This is also why Kalshi

and Polymarket both urgently updated their internal rules in March 2026, tightening self-regulation — trying

to prove the industry can govern itself before the bill passes.

Palantir Enters: AI Monitoring for Prediction Markets

On  March  10,  2026,  Polymarket  announced  a  surprising  partnership:  a  joint  deployment  with  Palantir  and

TWG AI of the Vergence AI engine to monitor sports prediction market trading activity.

Who is Palantir? The U.S. intelligence community's most trusted data analytics company — a go-to contractor

But this is precisely Polymarket's strategic choice. Under the twin pressures of congressional legislation and

For Polymarket, the U.S. market isn't a nice-to-have — it's the must-win main battlefield. ICE (NYSE's parent

company) has invested nearly $2 billion. MLB signed a 3-year, $300 million exclusive deal. Palantir deployed

an  AI  monitoring  system.  All  of  these  resources  point  toward  the  U.S.  market.  If  the  U.S.  app  can  roll  out

successfully, Polymarket gains a regulated, direct channel to tens of millions of American users — no longer

needing users to circumvent geoblocks with crypto wallets.

Sports: The New Growth Engine

Many people still think of prediction markets primarily as "election forecasting tools," but actual data tells a

completely different story.

On March 19, 2026, Polymarket announced it had become MLB's (Major League Baseball) exclusive prediction

market  partner.  $300  million  over  3  years  —  the  largest  sports  partnership  in  prediction  market  history.

Polymarket  gained  the  right  to  use  MLB  branding  and  access  to  Sportradar's  official  data  feeds.  MLB

simultaneously  signed  a  memorandum  of  understanding  (MOU)  with  the  CFTC  for  information  sharing,

establishing an unprecedented cooperation framework between a sports league and a financial regulator.

This wasn't the first. NHL (hockey), MLS (soccer), and UFC (mixed martial arts) had all previously partnered

with  prediction  market  platforms.  The  collective  pivot  from  sports  leagues  signals  a  realization:  prediction

markets  aren't  a  threat  —  they're  a  channel  that  can  generate  new  revenue  and  new  forms  of  fan

engagement.

On  Kalshi,  sports  already  account  for  roughly  90%  of  trading  volume.  Robinhood  has  launched  NFL  parlay

and  player  performance  betting.  Across  the  industry,  sports  account  for  over  60%  of  open  interest.  The

transformation  of  prediction  markets  from  "political  forecasting  tools"  to  "full-category  event  trading

platforms" is being driven primarily by sports.

Warning

But this puts prediction markets on a direct collision course with the traditional sports betting industry.

DraftKings, FanDuel, and their peers view prediction markets as existential threats. It's fundamentally the

same users doing the same thing, just through different regulatory channels. Prediction markets go through

the CFTC; traditional betting goes through state gambling commissions — and the former's compliance costs

are far lower. This is the root cause behind the 19 states suing Kalshi.

AI: From Tool to Leading Player

AI is fundamentally reshaping the prediction market ecosystem, and to a far greater degree than most people

realize.

One  key  data  point:  on  Polymarket's  public  leaderboard,  14  of  the  top  20  most  profitable  wallets  are

bots. That's 70%. This isn't a fringe phenomenon — the market's main actors have already changed.

Arbitrage traders extracted approximately $40 million in profit from Polymarket between April 2024 and April

2025.  Their  strategies  aren't  about  predicting  the  future  better  than  humans  —  they're  structural  arbitrage:

buying  both  Yes  and  No  when  their  combined  price  is  below  $1  to  lock  in  risk-free  profit;  exploiting  cross-

platform  price  gaps  between  Polymarket  and  Kalshi;  identifying  logical  inconsistencies  between  related

contracts.

Traditional Finance Enters: An Irreversible Trend

The  clearest  signal  from  2025-2026  is  the  stampede  of  traditional  financial  giants  into  the  space.  This  is  no

longer crypto's echo chamber.

ICE  (NYSE  parent  company)  has  invested  nearly  $2  billion  in  Polymarket  —  $1  billion  in  October  2025,

another  $600  million  in  March  2026.  The  NYSE's  parent  company  investing  in  a  crypto  prediction  market

would  have  been  unthinkable  two  years  ago.  This  isn't  just  a  bet  on  Polymarket  itself;  it's  a  strategic

positioning play by traditional exchanges for the "event contracts" asset class.

Robinhood, through integrating Kalshi contracts, put prediction markets directly in front of retail users. $100

million  in  annualized  revenue,  1  million+  users  participating.  Robinhood  proved  something:  the  growth

bottleneck for prediction markets isn't demand — it's the entry point. Embed it inside an app with 40 million

existing users, and they'll come on their own.

Bloomberg Terminal has already begun integrating prediction market data. Traders on Bloomberg can now

see Polymarket's probability quotes directly. Prediction markets are no longer a standalone sector — they're

embedding into the bedrock of global financial information.

Sports  leagues'  attitudes  have  also  shifted.  MLB,  NHL,  MLS,  and  UFC  are  collectively  embracing  prediction

markets.  These  leagues  were  once  among  the  most  adamant  opponents  of  any  form  of  event  wagering

(remember  Pete  Rose's  lifetime  ban).  Now  they're  proactively  signing  partnership  deals,  a  sign  that  the

legitimacy perception of the industry has undergone a fundamental transformation.

The Core Tension: Gambling or Information Tool?

Every  discussion  about  the  future  of  prediction  markets  ultimately  returns  to  the  same  question:  is  it

gambling, or is it information infrastructure?

This  isn't  a  philosophical  question  —  it's  a  legal  one.  Different  answers  mean  entirely  different  regulatory

frameworks and entirely different survival prospects.

The  CFTC  chose  the  "financial  derivative"  path,  granting  federal-level  legality.  But  the  bipartisan

congressional  "Prediction  Markets  are  Gambling  Act"  pursues  the  other  path:  classifying  election,

government,  war,  and  sports  contracts  outright  as  gambling.  If  passed,  Polymarket's  and  Kalshi's  core

businesses would be essentially wiped out.

The tension exists because both sides have a point.

The  case  for  information  tool:  In  the  2024  U.S.  election,  Polymarket's  forecast  outperformed  virtually  all

major  polls.  In  2025,  prediction  market  probability  movements  predicted  Federal  Reserve  decisions  1-2

weeks ahead of mainstream economists and polls. Brier score 0.06 vs. polling baseline 0.125 — academic data

supports prediction markets' superior accuracy.

The  case  for  gambling:  92.4%  of  participants  lose  money,  25%  of  volume  may  be  fake,  military  secrets  are

being  monetized  through  bets,  and  sports  dominate  trading  volume.  By  user  behavior,  most  people

participate in prediction markets in ways that aren't fundamentally different from playing slot machines.

This tension won't have a simple resolution. The most likely outcome: prediction markets remain legal at the

federal  level  but  face  category  restrictions;  sports  contracts  may  be  assigned  to  state-level  gambling

commissions, while political and economic contracts stay within the CFTC framework. A compromise — the

American way.

Closing Thoughts: A Few Words for the Reader

In writing this guide, my view of prediction markets went through several shifts.

First came excitement. A "truth machine" where people vote with real money, more accurate than polls, more

honest than experts — it sounded like the ultimate tool of the information age.

Then  came  caution.  UMA  manipulation,  military  intelligence  leaks,  92%  of  people  losing  money,  25%  fake

volume. The deeper I dug, the further this "truth machine" seemed from the ideal.

Finally came a pragmatic understanding. Prediction markets are not magic. They are a tool. And tools are

neither good nor bad — it depends on who uses them and how.

If you're considering participating, a few suggestions:

Key Insight

Observe first, then bet. Create an account and spend two weeks watching without trading. Observe how

prices move, the rhythm of popular markets, how settlement disputes are handled. Run mental paper trades

to see how accurate your own judgment is. If after two weeks you still want to play, start with money you

wouldn't mind losing entirely.

Key Insight

Treat it as an information source, not an ATM. The greatest value of prediction markets isn't in making you

money — it's in providing you with a crowd-calibrated probability signal. Want to know if the Fed will cut

rates next meeting? The probability on Polymarket is more useful than any single analyst's opinion. You

don't need to place a bet to access this value.

Key Insight

Control your positions, accept your losses. No single event should exceed 5% of your total capital. The

92.4% losing rate isn't a scare tactic — it's a statistical fact. If you can treat prediction markets as "tuition"

rather than "investment," your mindset will be much healthier.

Looking back at the history of prediction markets — from 16th-century Italian city-states' political wagering,

to  the  1988  Iowa  Electronic  Markets  experiment,  to  Polymarket's  birth  during  a  pandemic  in  2020,  to  the

industry's  push  toward  a  hundred-billion-dollar  scale  in  2026  —  it  has  always  been  about  the  same  thing:

humanity searching for better ways to quantify uncertainty.

The core insight of prediction markets is actually quite simple: when people back their judgments with real

money, information gets expressed more honestly and probabilities get priced more accurately. Hayek said it

in 1945: the price mechanism is the most efficient tool for aggregating dispersed knowledge. What's changed

is that technology has finally caught up with the theory.

The  future  of  this  market  depends  on  three  things:  how  Congress  legislates,  how  traditional  finance

integrates  it,  and  how  AI  transforms  it.  Regardless  of  which  direction  prevails,  prediction  markets'  role  as

"information infrastructure" is irreversible. Next time you see a flickering probability number on a Bloomberg

terminal or a news app, you're looking at more than just a number — you're seeing the collective judgment of

thousands of people who've voted with their wallets.

It  may  be  the  most  honest  information  tool  humanity  has  ever  created.  It  may  also  be  just  a  more

sophisticated form of gambling. In all likelihood, it's both.
